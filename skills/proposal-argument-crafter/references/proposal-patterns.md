# Proposal Patterns

## Use When

Load this reference for internal pitches, project proposals, architecture investments, vendor proposals, RFP responses, grant-like technical plans, and budget or staffing requests.

## Source Principles

- Compliance is persuasion in formal proposal contexts: reviewers use instructions and constraints to evaluate credibility.
- A proposal is a plan to address a problem, not a feature description.
- Persuasion depends on fit, feasibility, proof, and professionalism.
- Honesty is a technical communication requirement. Do not overstate certainty, benefits, qualifications, or schedule confidence.

## Constraint Extraction

Create a visible constraint list before drafting when the prompt includes an RFP, template, grading rubric, or stakeholder request.

```markdown
## Constraints
- Required sections:
- Exact heading names:
- Page or word limit:
- Formatting rules:
- Evaluation criteria:
- Submission format:
- Deadline:
- Required evidence:
- Disallowed content:
- Open questions:
```

## Argument Spine

Use this spine to keep the proposal coherent:

1. The current situation creates a cost, risk, delay, or missed opportunity.
2. The proposed plan addresses that situation better than the status quo.
3. The evidence shows why this plan, team, and timing fit the need.
4. The requested resources are proportionate to the value and risk.
5. The reviewer can approve a concrete next step.

## Audience Attitude

Tune the order of argument to the reviewer:

- Receptive audience: lead with the recommendation and supporting plan.
- Neutral audience: state the problem, then the recommendation and evidence.
- Skeptical or hostile audience: establish practical benefits, constraints, and proof before presenting the requested decision.

Answer likely objections directly and objectively. Do not hide them or rely on enthusiasm.

## Evidence Types

- Prior similar delivery.
- Prototype or benchmark.
- Customer, incident, support, or operations evidence.
- Cost of delay or risk exposure.
- Security, reliability, or compliance requirement.
- Named key personnel, team qualifications, and responsibilities.
- Available facilities, tooling, resources, and management structure when execution credibility depends on them.

## Assertion Emphasis

Make the proposal's main claims visible to skimming evaluators.

- Use assertion headings when the format allows custom headings.
- If headings are fixed by an RFP, use strong lead sentences or short callout lines instead.
- Do not let boilerplate credentials bury the proposal's central argument.

## Language and Format Checks

- Define unfamiliar terms and acronyms once; use the same term for each capability, deliverable, role, and risk throughout.
- Use active voice for commitments, responsibilities, and risk mitigations so reviewers can see who acts.
- Keep tables and lists parallel, especially budgets, milestones, deliverables, evidence, and risks.
- Replace hype with observable benefits, evidence, assumptions, or uncertainty.

## Schedule and Evaluation

- Use a milestone list or timeline for simple schedules.
- For complex schedules, use or describe a Gantt chart, network diagram, or dependency-aware timeline.
- State how the work will be evaluated: quality-control checks, acceptance criteria, formative reviews during the project, or summative review after completion.

## Risk Language

Prefer direct risk statements:

```markdown
Risk: [What could happen.]
Impact: [Why it matters.]
Mitigation: [What we will do.]
Decision point: [When we reassess.]
```

## Review Checklist

- Required instructions are satisfied exactly.
- The summary contains problem, solution, value, and ask.
- The proposal makes reviewer benefits explicit.
- The proposal preserves exact required headings when supplied.
- Likely reviewer objections are answered.
- Claims have evidence or are labeled as assumptions.
- Terms are consistent and acronyms are defined.
- Key personnel or execution qualifications are visible.
- Quality-control or evaluation methods are visible when delivery credibility depends on them.
- Main assertions stand out to skimming readers.
- Budget and schedule are concrete enough to evaluate.
- Major risks and mitigations are present.
- Lists and tables are parallel and easy to compare.
- The proposal asks for a specific decision.
